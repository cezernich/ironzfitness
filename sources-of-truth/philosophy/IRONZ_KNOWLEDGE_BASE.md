## 2. Equipment Guidance

### Bike Selection by Race Distance

**Sprint (12–15 mi bike):**
Road bike recommended. At 30–45 minutes on the bike, comfort, handling, and fast transitions matter more than aerodynamics. A TT bike is unnecessary for sprint racing.

**Olympic (25 mi bike):**
Either road or TT bike works. 60–90 minutes on the bike. Road bike gives more versatility for training; TT bike gives a small race-day edge.

**Half Ironman / 70.3 (56 mi bike):**
TT bike recommended. At 2.5–3.5 hours on the bike, the aero position saves meaningful time — typically 10–20 minutes vs. a road bike at the same effort. If budget allows, this is where a TT bike starts paying for itself.

**Full Ironman (112 mi bike):**
TT bike strongly recommended. 5–7 hours on the bike. Aerodynamic savings compound over this distance — often 30–60+ minutes vs. a road bike at the same effort. Invest in a proper bike fit first. This is the single biggest equipment decision for Ironman racing.

**Base building / no race:**
Road bike is the most versatile training bike. Works for group rides, varied terrain, all race distances. If planning to race 70.3 or Ironman, consider a TT bike when ready. Clip-on aero bars on a road bike are a solid intermediate step.

**Budget priority order (if buying one bike):**
1. Road bike with proper fit — covers everything
2. Add clip-on aero bars — cheap aero gains for long course
3. TT/tri bike — when committed to 70.3+ racing

### Wetsuit Guidance

**When to wear one:**
- Water temp below 76°F / 24.5°C — wetsuits allowed in most sanctioned races
- Water temp 76–84°F / 24.5–29°C — wetsuits allowed but no age-group awards (USAT rules)
- Water temp above 84°F / 29°C — wetsuits prohibited

**What to buy:**
- Full-sleeve triathlon-specific wetsuit for most athletes
- Sleeveless if shoulder flexibility is limited or water is warm
- Do NOT use a surfing wetsuit — wrong thickness, wrong flexibility, will slow you down

### Running Shoes

- Get fitted at a running specialty store — shoe type depends on gait, not brand
- Rotate 2+ pairs to extend shoe life and reduce injury risk
- Replace every 300–500 miles
- Carbon-plated race shoes (e.g., Nike Vaporfly, Saucony Endorphin Pro) are legal in triathlon and provide measurable benefit for half and full Ironman run legs
- Train in your race shoes enough to know they work for you — never race in new shoes

### Helmet

- Any CPSC-certified helmet is legal for all triathlon distances
- Aero helmets save 30–60 seconds per hour on the bike but are hotter and less ventilated
- For sprint/Olympic: standard road helmet is fine
- For 70.3/Ironman: aero helmet is worth considering if budget allows and conditions aren't extreme heat

## 3. Nutrition — Race Day Quick Reference

(Detailed nutrition philosophy is in IronZ_Nutrition_Philosophy_v1.docx. This section covers the most commonly asked race-day questions.)

### Pre-Race Meal
- 3–4 hours before race start
- 1–2 g/kg carbohydrate, low fiber, low fat
- Examples: white rice + banana, oatmeal + honey, bagel + peanut butter
- Familiar foods only — nothing new on race day

### During Race — Carb Targets per Hour

| Distance | Bike (g/hr) | Run (g/hr) |
|----------|-------------|------------|
| Sprint | 0–30 | 0 (too short) |
| Olympic | 30–60 | 0–30 |
| Half Ironman | 60–80 | 40–60 |
| Full Ironman | 80–100+ | 40–60 |

### Hydration During Race
- 500–800 ml per hour on the bike (weather dependent)
- 400–600 ml per hour on the run
- Include electrolytes — sodium target: 500–1000 mg per hour in hot conditions
- Practice in training — gut tolerance is trainable

### Common Mistakes
- Starting too fast (especially the swim — adrenaline)
- Not eating enough on the bike — you bonk on the run
- Trying new nutrition on race day
- Underestimating heat — add 200 ml/hr to hydration plan in hot conditions
- Skipping aid stations early in the run

## 4. Training FAQs

### "How many hours per week should I train?"

| Race Distance | Beginner | Intermediate | Advanced |
|--------------|----------|--------------|----------|
| Sprint | 4–6 hrs | 5–8 hrs | 6–10 hrs |
| Olympic | 6–8 hrs | 8–12 hrs | 10–15 hrs |
| Half Ironman | 8–10 hrs | 10–14 hrs | 12–18 hrs |
| Full Ironman | 10–14 hrs | 14–18 hrs | 16–25 hrs |

These include all three sports + strength. Quality over quantity — a focused 8-hour week beats a junk 14-hour week.

### "Can I do triathlon and lift heavy?"

Yes, especially in the offseason and base-building phase. Key principles:
- Lift 2–4 days per week depending on phase
- Prioritize compound movements (squat, deadlift, bench, row, overhead press)
- Reduce lifting volume (not intensity) as race approaches
- Functional strength helps triathlon performance: core stability, hip strength, shoulder endurance
- Hypertrophy is fine in the offseason — extra muscle mass doesn't hurt sprint/Olympic performance
- For Ironman: lean toward strength-endurance (higher rep, moderate weight) in the build phase

### "Should I train all three sports every week?"

In base building: yes, hit each sport at least twice per week to build sport-specific fitness. In race-specific phases: prioritize your weakest discipline and maintain the others. In recovery weeks (deload): reduce volume 40–50% across all sports.

### "How long should my base building phase be?"

- First-time triathletes: 8–16 weeks of base before any race-specific work
- Experienced athletes returning: 4–8 weeks
- Offseason after a race season: 4–6 weeks easy/unstructured, then 8–12 weeks base
- Base building has no fixed end date — you can stay in base phase until a race appears on the calendar

### "What's more important — swim, bike, or run?"

Time spent on bike leg is the largest portion of any triathlon. Improvements in cycling typically yield the biggest time savings. However, the run is where races are won or lost — many athletes fade on the run due to poor pacing or nutrition. Swim is the smallest time contributor but technique improvements have outsized returns for beginners.

**Priority for beginners:** Swim technique > Bike fitness > Run endurance
**Priority for intermediate:** Bike power > Run speed > Swim endurance
**Priority for advanced:** Weakest discipline > Bike power > Run speed

### "Do I need a coach?"

Not required but helpful. IronZ is designed to give you structured training without a coach. Consider a coach if:
- Training for your first Ironman
- Plateaued after 2+ years of self-coaching
- Recovering from injury and need guidance
- Budget allows ($150–400/month typical)

## 5. Swim-Specific Knowledge

### Pool Etiquette
- Circle swim (counterclockwise in the US) when sharing lanes
- Tap the foot of the person ahead to pass; they stop at the wall and let you go
- Don't push off directly in front of someone

### Open Water vs. Pool
- Open water sighting: lift head every 6–8 strokes to sight
- Drafting in open water is legal and saves 15–25% energy — swim on someone's hip or feet
- Practice in open water at least 4–6 times before a race
- Wetsuit adds buoyancy — you'll swim 5–10% faster than pool times

### CSS (Critical Swim Speed)
- The most reliable threshold metric for swim training
- Test: all-out 400m time and all-out 200m time
- CSS = (400m distance - 200m distance) / (400m time - 200m time)
- Retest every 8–12 weeks (or per the 90-day threshold refresh)
- Used by IronZ to auto-classify swim level and set training zones

## 6. Cycling-Specific Knowledge

### FTP (Functional Threshold Power)
- Estimated max power sustainable for 1 hour
- Test: 20-minute all-out effort × 0.95 = estimated FTP
- Retest every 8–12 weeks
- Used by IronZ to auto-classify cycling level and set training zones
- Requires a power meter ($200–800) or smart trainer

### Indoor vs. Outdoor Training
- Indoor (trainer) is more time-efficient: no coasting, no stops, no traffic
- Outdoor develops bike handling, cornering, group riding skills
- Mix both: 1–2 indoor structured sessions + 1 outdoor ride per week is ideal
- Indoor RPE feels harder — adjust expectations (indoor FTP is often 5–10% lower)

### Aero Position
- Single biggest speed gain in cycling — more impactful than lighter bike or better wheels
- Get a bike fit before spending money on equipment
- Practice aero position in training — it uses different muscles
- Start with 10–15 minutes in aero, build to full ride duration

## 7. Running-Specific Knowledge

### Threshold Pace
- Pace you can sustain for approximately 60 minutes in a race
- Roughly correlates to recent 10K race pace or half marathon pace + 15–20 sec/mile
- Retest every 8–12 weeks
- Used by IronZ to auto-classify running level and set training zones

### Injury Prevention
- Increase weekly mileage by no more than 10% per week
- Include at least 1 rest or cross-training day between hard run days
- 80% of running should be at easy/conversational pace
- Strength training (especially glutes, hips, core) reduces running injury risk by up to 50%
- Replace shoes every 300–500 miles

### Brick Workouts
- Bike-to-run transition workout — essential for triathlon training
- Start with short bricks: 30 min bike → 10 min run
- Build to race-simulation bricks: full bike duration → 20–30 min run
- Legs feel heavy and awkward for the first 5–10 minutes — this is normal and trainable
- Do 1 brick per week in the build phase, every 2 weeks in base

## 8. Strength for Triathletes

### Why Lift
- Reduces injury risk across all three sports
- Improves power output (cycling watts, run speed, swim pull strength)
- Builds bone density — important for endurance athletes
- Improves body composition when combined with proper nutrition
- Does NOT make you "too bulky" for endurance — that's a myth at triathlon training volumes

### Functional Strength Pairings

When strength is paired with a cardio sport on the same day, exercise selection should complement the sport:

| Same-Day Sport | Strength Focus | Key Exercises |
|---------------|----------------|---------------|
| Swim | Pull + Core | Rows, pull-ups, lat pulldowns, face pulls, pallof press, dead bugs |
| Bike | Legs + Posterior chain | Squats, lunges, leg press, Romanian deadlift, calf raises, hip bridges |
| Run | Single-leg + Hips + Core | Single-leg deadlift, step-ups, lateral band walks, Copenhagen plank, Bulgarian split squat |
| Rest day (standalone) | Full body or weak points | Compound lifts + accessories for lagging areas |

### Periodization with Triathlon Training

| Training Phase | Strength Approach | Sets × Reps | Days/Week |
|---------------|-------------------|-------------|-----------|
| Offseason / Base | Hypertrophy + Max Strength | 3–4 × 8–12 (hypertrophy), 4–5 × 3–5 (strength) | 3–4 |
| Build | Strength-Endurance | 3 × 10–15 | 2–3 |
| Peak / Race Prep | Maintenance only | 2 × 6–8 | 1–2 |
| Race Week | None or very light | — | 0 |
| Recovery | Light, movement-focused | 2 × 12–15 | 1–2 |
